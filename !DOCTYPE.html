The Misrepresentation of Nuclear Energy

In 2026, debates on energy intensify as we collectively decide how to fuel our growing society. Nuclear energy is unfairly eliminated from these conversations due to a wrongfully perceived danger, when in reality, nuclear energy is very safe. We propose changes to the school curriculum to destroy nuclear prejudice and thus open more equitable and informed debates on the topic.

01 / State the Problem

Where the fear comes from

Almost nobody has stood inside a working reactor or read a real safety report. Due to that, it is commonly misconceived that nuclear reactors operate under casual conditions, when in reality they are very strictly regulated (Galindo).

02 / The Evidence

What the safety data shows

Nuclear energy is not as deadly as claimed. While sources like coal produce 26 deaths per kilowatt hour produced, nuclear claims only 0.03 lives. Nuclear death rates are exaggerated due to isolated incidents such as Chernobyl and Hiroshima/Nagasaki (“What Do Americans Really Think about the Bombing of Hiroshima And”).

03 / Why It Matters

Nuclear and the world's growing energy needs

The case for nuclear is not only about safety. It is also about how much power the world needs. We use more energy now than ever, and the demand keeps rising.

The world’s energy demand is projected to increase by 25% in 2050. To meet these needs and counteract justified de-investment in fossil fuels, all renewable sources need to be carefully considered. Nucelar is shown to have the potential to meet these needs (Exonmobil).

False rejection of nuclear energy overly complicates our energy problems. If nuclear energy can be successfully scaled with popular support. It can be a large component to the solution in its current, let alone future, state (ExxonMobil).

04 / Inform the Public

How nuclear energy works

Nuclear energy can physically be derived from fusion and fission reactions. These reactions are not well understood by the general public despite being taught in typical chemistry classes. 100% of Earth's nuclear energy is from fission reactions, though fusion reactions show increasing potential for the near future (Galindo).

In use today

Fission

Splits heavy uranium atoms into smaller ones. Every nuclear plant on Earth right now runs on fission.

100%

of the world's nuclear power today

Coming soon

Fusion

Fuses light atoms together, the same process that powers the sun. Not commercial yet, but moving fast.

~4×

the energy of fission per unit of fuel, and millions of times the energy of coal or oil by volume

The fission chain reaction

Fission starts by a neutron colliding and splitting an atom into two smaller ones that release 2-3 neutrons to continue the chain reaction. The bonds breaking release energy (Galindo).

From heat to electricity

The heat derived from the chemical bonds breaking during fission creates steam in surrounding water that powers a turbine. Cooling liquid is heated, which results in different steam leaving the power plant (Galindo).

05 / The Biggest Fear, Examined

Radiation fear and what it actually costs

A common protest against nuclear energy is the fear of Acute Radiation Poisoning (ARP). The fear often causes more damage than the radiation (Bruce Hayes, 7).

ARP is characterized as short intense exposure to sources of D.N.A damaging information. Fear of ARP is also derived from the linear no threshold hypothesis (LNT), which proclaims that any exposure to nuclear energy induces a linear increasing risk of ARP and cancer for a unit increase in time and radiation strength.

Such theories gained popularity after the Chernobyl and Fukushima disasters. It is widely agreed upon that LNT holds little scientific basis and is only followed for safety (Bruce Hayes, 3).

Hayes calls this radiophobia, "the clinical fear of ionizing radiation," and it carries real costs (Hayes). It leads people to skip X-rays they actually need and makes some lab work harder to keep sterile. Fukushima is the clearest example. No member of the public died from radiation there, though one plant worker's later death was officially linked to it. What killed people was the evacuation: by 2020 about 2,300 evacuees had died from its stress and disruption, most of them elderly (World Nuclear Association, "Fukushima Daiichi Accident"). Chernobyl and nuclear weapons are different stories, where real radiation harm did happen.

06 / The Other Big Worry

What happens to nuclear waste

Nuclear waste is another common fear regarding nuclear energy discussions. While select accidents have occurred, nuclear waste is arguably better controlled than industrial waste (Galindo).

Nuclear reactants (U235) have the potential to be reused for 3-5 years. After this point, they are considered a very high-risk radioactive pollutant, which must be buried or locked away, usually in solid steel and concrete dry casks like the one shown here (Galindo).

The Waste Isolation Plant in New Mexico is the only waste management "accident" in the United States and was resolved with minimal health complications (Jennings). Internationally, countries with weaker regulatory policies have faced more accidents. There has never been a case of waste leaking during transportation; therefore, the general public has never been at risk due to nuclear power plant waste (or nuclear healthcare or lab waste at that) (Galindo).

09 / Take Action

What we can do about it

Clearing up the confusion around nuclear energy takes two things working together: more honest, up to date science education, and nuclear organizations that actually engage the communities around them.

OUR SOLUTION

Teach the truth early, and get the plants talking to their towns

To clear up any misconceptions regarding nuclear energy, we as people must not only integrate more proactive education in environmental sciences in middle school and high school, but also nuclear organizations themselves should advocate for the increase in community engagement and support in nuclear energy, positively impacting our present and possibly future generation of clean nuclear power.

FRONT ONE / EDUCATION

Education in Youth

Revamping the curriculum in education as early as middle school is essential to cement the truth regarding nuclear power in today's society. A study in the University of Louisiana explains that educational processes should stay current with modern trends within the fields of science and common learning. If the current curriculum doesn't employ the required technologies or methods of teaching modern-day events, the education system must be revamped. (Why Curriculum and Instruction Change Is Important).

  • Petition the local Board of Education to add an energy and radiation unit to middle school science.
  • Push for science standards that cover modern technology, not just the basics.
FRONT TWO / COMMUNITY

Get the plants talking to their towns

Nuclear organizations themselves must advocate for the integration of nuclear energy and common knowledge relating to such in their community. These organizations can host transparent sessions with their town, offering things like jobs, as a statistic shows that nuclear power plants supply around half a million jobs in the US, and they contribute billions of dollars to the economy through federal and state tax revenues. (U.S. Department of Energy)

  • Ask local operators to host regular open houses and Q and A sessions.
  • Lead with jobs, tax revenue, and transparency, not technical jargon.
THE MAIN AUDIENCE

Education

As early as middle school, children are the target audience for education in nuclear energy. Graphs and trends in society point to younger citizens being more eager to learn compared to old people. (Eric Hanushek) As people get older and wrinklier, they're usually either less able to learn new things or just unwilling to. As a result, is probably easier to imbed the right values relating to nuclear energy while people are still young.

Marginal gains in literacy and numeracy fall steadily as people age, which is part of why early education is the better target (Hanushek et al.).

THE SECOND AUDIENCE

Reach the power plants

The nuclear power organizations are also a valid target audience to get them to raise funding in whatever means to get the general public to view nuclear energy as a safe and viable option. Endowing money to such companies could allow them to increase public awareness to the people already out of school, such as facts regarding safe living near power plants or new acts relating to them. (Kati Austgen)

WHY BOTHER

Benefits

  • Nuclear energy is our largest source of clean energy, yearly generating enough electricity to power 72 million homes. Advocating for nuclear energy increases clean electricity in our nation overall.
  • Domestic nuclear plants can employ up to 800 individuals with salaries fifty percent greater than other energy generation sources. Supporting nuclear plants helps individuals in today’s society with employment.
  • General public information regarding nuclear energy is just good for our society in general; raising average common knowledge within a community is better.

The Other Side and Sources

Counterargument and Sources

The other side of the debate

Nuclear power isn't perfect, and critics have actual problems. Plant building is expensive and takes a long time. Uranium mining also harms the environment. Some people might argue that funding money could go toward clean energy instead. This project does not say nuclear is flawless, but it argues that nuclear is often seen as more dangerous than the evidence shows (U.S. Department of Energy).
